who we are

Xenophos is the result of the ongoing collaboration between Alex Lenail and Bradford Thorne, and a number of student artists from and around Palo Alto High School in California. Armed with home-made light apparatuses, we've sought to prove that lightpainting is more than just a curiosity but an art in its own right. Meet us...

what we do

Lightpainting is long exposure photography in a dark context with interpretive waving of lights. Cameras typically capture moments with a shutter opening for a fraction of a second, but when that same shutter is opened for much longer spans of time, one image can capture trails of light, brushstrokes to a lightpainter. How
It's
Done

Lightpainting is exactly what it sounds like: painting with light.

What makes it dissimilar from regular painting is that instead of being confined to some rectangular two-dimensional space, lightpainting is inherently a 3 dimensional art (much more alike to dancing than drawing). In lightpainting, the world is your canvas, and the brushstrokes are any sort of motion you can come up with.

Lightpainting can also be conceived in part as the antithesis of what you might do with oils or watercolor: they depend on light to take on meaning and form, whereas lightpainting only ever works in the dark. A painting in an unlit space isn't a painting to the viewer, and a lightpainting exposed to daylight is just some crazies waving sticks around. But enough metaphor, what is it actually?

Lightpainting is long exposure photography in a dark setting, paired with light in motion during the span of the shot. The basic anatomy of a lightpainting shoot is a photographer with a camera on a tripod set to optimized parameters taking a 10-30 second photo of one or more lightpainters waving light-tools or "brushes" within the frame and timeframe of the shot. I'm going to break that down for you below to give you an idea of each of the important pieces before you can go out and do it yourself.

Lights

Every part of lightpainting is in some way an art, and choosing your tools is no different. We at Xenophos built our own tools, but not from scratch. There are a few categories of tools which we built, described below.

The Light Sticks

These are the some of the simplest and best tools. We bought the lights for about $5 apiece at Fry's electronics and attached them with duct tape to some old wood we cut and sanded to our liking.


The Light Staves

These are the tools most unique to our work which took a long time to put together. We started with pairs of Cold Cathodes and wired them up to sets of 8 AA batteries each, and a switch, which took a lot of soldering. Then we mounted those structures with hot glue and duct tape onto long rectangular pieces of wood, and added a duct-tape handle.

The EL Whip

Electrofluorescent wire is used for plenty of lighting applications. In our case, we took a strand of it and cut it into sections, soldered them all together to take power from one source, and mounted that onto a piece of wood which all together resembled a short EL 'whip' of 12 18-inch strands of wire. When we shake them in a shot, they look like static fire.

The Steel Wool Spinner

This tool is made from a whisk attached to a cooking spoon by some wire. We put the steel wool (which you can find at your local hardware store) in the whisk and light it with a 9V battery and spin the sparks out. Be careful not to use this tool near highly flammable things! The spinning generates a lot of sparks. Wear Jeans and a sweatshirt when using this tool -- the sparks sting.

The Floodlight

This tool is used to light up the scene entirely, typically for a very brief moment, in order to capture the way things are before changing them. This process is kind of like a camera flash, and in lightpainting, it's a way of cheating by actually getting a static image and then painting on top of it.

The Sphere Tool

This tool is a few LEDs attached to a string attached to a handle, which when spun while walking in a circle, generates a beautiful orb of color. We've also done this with a fiber optic light instead of an LED at the end of the string.

The Lightsaber

We didn't modify this tool at all, because it worked exactly the way we wanted it to. Take out those old lightsabers from your childhood and use them to paint with light!

Action

Each of the tools comes with an implicit effect, and that effect is made by using the tool in the particular way it's built to be used. However, do not be constrained by the way we learned to use our tools. Functional fixedness is not a part of lightpainting, and we discover new ways to use our tools as we go.

In any case, below I'll describe how we used our tools, and you can borrow our ideas or come up with your own.

Spinning steel wool

1.  Take the steel wool out of the bag and rip it apart a little, for better oxygen flow.

2.  Put the steel wool in the whisk. Set the focus for the camera to when you will be spinning. Stand right behind that location.

3.  Light the steel wool with a 9V battery by rubbing the battery electrodes against the wool. For best results spend about two seconds rubbing both sides of the wool.

4.  Start spinning the wool and start the camera at the same time. Spin hard, but not as hard as you can. Try to stay motionless and only spin your wrist/forearm. Spin the wool in front of you, not right next to you.

Orbs

1.  Set the camera focus.

2.  Start spinning before you take the photo, with your dominant arm offsef from your body.

3.  Once you start the shot, begin walking in a circle arounf your hand. Try to keep your hand over the same spot on the ground the entire time.

4.  Walk until you've completed a half circle, or a full circle for a brighter orb.

Freeform light staves

1.  Set focus.

2.  Use two light staves interpretively. The less you move the more clear you will be in the shot. Release your inner Jedi!

Arachnids

1.  Set the focus.

2.  Have someone stand in the center and someone with the floodlight outside of the camera frame but capable of lighting the person up.

3.  Start the shot and have the person being the arachnid slowly lift their arms around them while the person with the floodlight strobes the light the desired number of times.

Setting

Nature

Go out into the woods and paint gentle light around trees, meadows, and streams.

Underground

We found an extraordinary setting in some local abandoned tunnels plastered with graffiti, which gave our paintings an implicit frame.

Urban

These are hard to find but look incredible when lightpainted against. Many of our inspirations (below) make incredible use of these settings.

People


Just one last note: we believe that lightpainting doesn't lend itself too well to solo work. It's a great deal more powerful and meaningful done with others.

On one level, you need someone behind the camera and someone painting, and performing both at once is challenging. On another level, the complexity of the shot is limited when you only have one painter. But most importantly, art is meant to be shared. It's a vehicle to explore another's consciousness, a way to connect. I've been amazed by some of the paintings my friends had hidden away inside their heads, expressed in a sort of unique dance, a cognitive fingerprint of theirs, of which the traces are superimposed onto one another in the form of an image. We've had so much fun doing this together, and you should too!
